She didn't answer him. But her shoulders dropped a quarter inch — the kind of release that happens below the level of decision, the body letting go of something the mind hasn't named yet.

They walked.

The gallery stretched ahead of them, wide and low-ceilinged, the mako glow softening as they moved away from the reactor core's housing. Their footsteps found a rhythm without either of them setting it — hers light and precise, his steady and grounded, the katana's saya whispering against his hakama with each stride. Somewhere behind them, muffled by distance and concrete and the reactor's own labored breathing, the sweep teams continued their grid. Someone else's problem now. Someone else's story.

The vent access was a blast door set into the gallery's far wall — heavy, industrial, painted in faded yellow hazard stripes that had been scuffed by years of maintenance carts. Tifa pulled the manual release without breaking stride, one hand on the lever, weight behind it, and the door groaned open on hydraulics that needed servicing. Beyond it, a short concrete tunnel angled upward, and at its end—

Air.

Real air. Or what passed for it beneath the plate — warm and close and carrying the permanent mineral tang of recycled atmosphere, threaded with cooking smoke and machine oil and the distant sweetness of something growing stubbornly in bad soil. But after the reactor's chemical breath, it hit Autumn's lungs like cold water. The difference between breathing inside a wound and breathing outside one.

They emerged onto a maintenance access platform bolted to the reactor's exterior shell, three stories above the slum rooftops. Below them, Sector 5 spread out in its cramped, improbable sprawl — corrugated metal and salvage timber and electric light strung between buildings on cables that sagged like tired arms. The plate hung overhead, vast and absolute, close enough to feel its weight even though it was hundreds of meters up. No sky. Just the underside of someone else's city, pressing down on this one like a hand on a chest.

But people lived here. That was the thing. Laundry hung from lines. A dog barked somewhere. A food stall's lantern swung in a draft that shouldn't have existed but did, throwing warm light across a street where two kids were arguing over something too small to see from this height.

Tifa leaned against the platform railing. Both hands. Head down for a moment — just a moment — while the reactor's noise faded behind them and the slums' noise rose to replace it. A different frequency. Human-scaled. Livable.

When she straightened, her expression had reorganized itself. The crisis face was gone. What replaced it was something more measured — still alert, still working, but no longer running a countdown. She looked out over the rooftops, and Autumn saw her eyes track a route through the sector that she clearly knew by heart. Landmarks only she could read. A particular water tower. A gap between buildings. The geography of a home she wasn't ready to name to a stranger.

Then she looked at him. Really looked, for the first time since the junction. Taking inventory now that the light was better and the clock wasn't screaming.

The sword. The earth-toned clothing that didn't belong to any culture she recognized in Midgar. The topknot. The calm. The way he stood on a platform three stories up with reactor debris in his hair and looked like he was exactly as comfortable as he'd be anywhere else.

"So," she said. Her voice had settled back into something warmer — not the clipped efficiency of the corridors, but not open either. Careful. The tone of someone deciding how much door to leave ajar. "You're not Shinra. You're not from around here. And you carry a sword like you know what to do with it."

A beat. Below them, the food stall's lantern swung.

"I'm not going to ask why you were in that reactor. That's your business." She crossed her arms. Not defensive — organizing. "But you saw things in there that certain people would want to ask you about. On both sides."

She let that land.

"So the question is — where are you trying to go?"
